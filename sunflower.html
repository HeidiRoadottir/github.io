Det man ikke ser – Sunflower Lanyard & Stigma

Master semester project · Critical & speculative design · Disability & stigma

The sunflower lanyard is meant to discreetly signal that the wearer has an invisible disability and may need extra understanding. This project investigates how the lanyard is actually interpreted in public space – and how it can both support and stigmatise the people who wear it.

Critical design Speculative design Research-through-design Stigma & disability studies Embodiment

Context & problem framing

The sunflower lanyard has spread widely as a symbol for hidden disabilities. Many wearers and relatives describe it as helpful – but it also comes with ambiguous reactions: curious stares, misunderstandings, or feelings of not wanting to “out” oneself in public.

Our group has personal connections to the lanyard, which informed the project’s motivation: to explore how it is read by others, and how it shapes the embodied experience of being visible as “different”.

My role

  • Co-developed the problem framing around visibility, stigma and control.
  • Contributed to the design and execution of critical design experiments.
  • Participated in analysis of responses and experiential reflections.
  • Helped connect theory (e.g. Goffman, Garland-Thomson) to design outcomes.

Design experiments

Exploratory experiments

Early experiments explored how people perceive images of sunflower lanyard wearers, what assumptions they make, and how the lanyard functions as a social symbol. Surveys and visual materials were used to surface stereotypes.

“Boxed stigma” & public interventions

Later prototypes functioned as “social mirrors”: critical artefacts and setups that intentionally made staring, judgement and discomfort more visible. These were tested in semi-public contexts to observe reactions and facilitate discussion about who has the power to look and to label.

Outcome & contribution

Instead of proposing a “better lanyard”, the project uses design to:

  • Expose the ambivalence of visibility for people with hidden disabilities.
  • Show how the sunflower symbol can both protect and mark out the wearer.
  • Invite reflection on who is allowed to stare, interpret and define others.

The work positions critical design as a way of negotiating social norms rather than solving them, contributing to discussions on inclusive design and disability representation.

Reflections & learnings

This project deepened my understanding of how design can be used as a research method in sensitive topics. It also sharpened my awareness of the ethics involved when working with symbols tied to vulnerability and identity.
